When Newcrest Mining's Cadia East mine in New South Wales faced unexpected grade variability in 2019, production forecasts missed by 15%, costing the company $180 million in lost revenue. The culprit wasn't poor geology—it was inadequate spatial modeling that failed to capture grade uncertainty between drillholes.

This tutorial covers ore grade forecasting with machine learning: Ordinary Kriging for baseline spatial interpolation, Gaussian Process Regression combining geochemical covariates with spatial patterns for 16% accuracy gains and calibrated uncertainty (94.8% confidence coverage), XGBoost achieving lowest MAE through non-parametric ensemble learning, and spatial cross-validation preventing overfitting.

This matters because drillholes give point samples but mines need continuous grade maps. When you add machine learning to geochemical covariates (Cu, As, Fe, S, Pb), you unlock probabilistic forecasts that reveal not just where the gold is, but where your predictions are most uncertain—critical intelligence for adaptive drilling and pit design.
